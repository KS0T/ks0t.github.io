I got involved with amateur radio rather late in life, at least compared to some people who get into it when they're kids. I was in my mid-40s, and while I'd been curious about it, and known several "hams", I had never quite gotten around to doing anything about it. I wasn't entirely sure what I would use it for, and most of the hams I knew had gotten licensed as young people, and then never used it much. Even those who kept going with it didn't operate a lot - mainly on field days or the like.

I started to reconsider getting licensed when I started to get serious about disaster preparedness, however. Where I live is subject to considerable seismic activity, and we even have volcanoes nearby, and the local authorities and folks like the USGS have been warning people in the PNW for years to be ready for "the big one" which has a small but serious chance of occuring sometime soon.

The likely result of that happening would be very bad - large parts of my city damaged, no clean water, no food, no power, and certainly no internet or phone service, for weeks if not longer. Given this, I started to plan and learn more, and when it came to communications in such a scenario, amateur radio seemed like exactly what was needed.

It was shortly after that I ran into an old acquaintance at the DEFCON hacking conference in Las Vegas, and she encouraged me to get licensed. She is part of a team that runs license exams at that convention.

I took her up on the challenge, and got my Technician and General licenses in one go in 2018, and about a year later, got my Extra. Since then, I've been learning all I can about this huge and fascinating hobby, and this site is where I'm sharing my experiences and experiments. I hope you find something of use to you here.

73,

Malcolm, KS0T
